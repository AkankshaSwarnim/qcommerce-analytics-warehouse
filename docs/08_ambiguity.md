# Was the substitution any good?

*This is the chapter about a question the data cannot answer.*

Every number in this document is generated by `src/analysis.py` and lives in
`reports/results.json`. Nothing here is typed by hand.

---

## The question, as it arrived

> "We substitute on about 40% of stock-outs. Are we doing it well?"

Reasonable question. Obvious next step: measure substitution quality, put it on
a dashboard, set a target.

Except there is no such thing as substitution quality in this warehouse. There
is no satisfaction field, no thumbs-up, no rating, no complaint linkage. Eight
source systems, 1.1 million order-items, and **not one byte anywhere records
whether the customer was okay with what arrived.**

The picker's swap is logged. The customer's reaction is not.

So the honest first answer is: *the question as posed has no answer here.* That
is not the end of the analysis. It is the beginning of it.

---

## Decomposing an unanswerable question

The question cannot be answered. It can be **replaced** with questions that can
be — and the replacement is where the judgement lives. It is not a technicality
to wave through in a footnote; it is the analysis.

| Asked | Answerable? | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Was the customer satisfied? | **No** | Nothing records satisfaction. No proxy recovers it. |
| Did they keep it? | Yes | Acceptance is logged. |
| Did they come back? | Yes | Derivable from order sequence. |
| Which swap type does best? | Yes | Type is logged; outcomes are comparable across types. |
| How much damage does a swap repair? | **No** | Requires the counterfactual: what this customer would have done had the item been in stock. |

Two of those five are unanswerable, and they are the two the question was
actually about. That has to be said out loud, early, to the person who asked —
not buried in a limitations section after they have already screenshotted the
number.

---

## Three proxies, chosen to disagree

Each is defensible. Each is a different bet about what "good" means. I built all
three rather than picking one, because picking one quietly is how a judgement
call gets laundered into a fact.

### Proxy A — acceptance as consent
> *A substitution worked if the customer accepted it.*

**Contrast:** accepted vs offered-but-declined.
**Result:** **+3.49 pp** on 30-day repeat.

**Blind spot:** acceptance is not endorsement. A customer at 11pm with no
alternative accepts whatever arrives. This proxy cannot distinguish approval
from resignation — and the contrast is confounded by *choice*: customers who
decline may simply be the ones with options.

### Proxy B — retention as forgiveness
> *A substitution worked if they ordered again within 30 days.*

**Contrast:** accepted-substitute vs no-substitute-offered.
**Result:** **+4.07 pp**.

**Blind spot:** whether a substitute was *offered* is a picker decision, not a
coin flip. This compares situations where staff chose to act against ones where
they did not — selection on the treatment itself. And 30-day repeat is
downstream of everything: price, habit, weather, a competitor's promo. The
signal from one swap is a whisper in a stadium.

### Proxy C — swap distance
> *A substitution worked in proportion to how close the replacement was.*

**Result:** ranges from **+1.10 pp** to **+5.70 pp** depending on swap type.

**Blind spot:** this one is not an outcome measure at all. It encodes a *belief
about groceries* dressed as a metric. It will look correct exactly whenever the
belief is correct, which is not the same as being evidence. Stated plainly
rather than smuggled in.

---

## They disagree, and the disagreement is the finding

| Proxy | Estimate |
|---|---|
| A — acceptance | +3.49 pp |
| B — retention | +4.07 pp |
| C — best swap | +5.70 pp |
| C — worst swap | +1.10 pp |

**Spread: 4.6 pp.**

Four defensible analyses, four different answers, on the same data. Reporting
any one of them as *"the impact of substitutions"* would be choosing a number
and calling it a measurement.

If the deliverable were a single figure on a dashboard, this project would be
finished and wrong.

---

## What survives: the ranking

The magnitude is proxy-dependent. **The order is not.**

Re-derived under two control groups chosen because they have *opposite*
selection problems — one holds the picker's decision fixed, the other holds the
customer's choice fixed. Agreement between them is worth something precisely
because they do not share a confounder:

| Swap type | vs declined | vs no offer | n |
|---|---|---|---|
| same_brand_diff_size | **+5.13** | **+5.70** | 5,538 |
| diff_brand_same_product | **+2.54** | **+3.11** | 4,734 |
| diff_product_same_cat | **+0.52** | **+1.10** | 1,523 |

**Order stable across proxies: yes.**

And the injected ground truth — which `analysis.py` is mechanically forbidden
from seeing, and which `validate.py` checks only afterwards — was:

```
same_brand_diff_size      repair 0.82
diff_brand_same_product   repair 0.47
diff_product_same_cat     repair 0.11
```

Same order. **The ranking is recovered; the magnitude never is.**

---

## The asymmetry that makes this useful

> **Magnitude: unrecoverable. Ranking: robust.**

This is not a consolation prize. The business decision was never a magnitude
decision. Nobody is going to do anything differently on hearing "substitutions
recover +4.07% of the damage." The decision is: **what do we tell pickers to do
when the shelf is empty?**

That is a ranking question. And the ranking holds under every proxy tested.

So the data cannot support the headline number, and fully supports the decision.
An analyst who reports the magnitude is reporting an artefact of their own proxy
choice. An analyst who reports the ranking is reporting something the business
can act on tomorrow morning.

---

## The one number worth escalating

The weakest swap — same category, different product (milk → laban) — has a
confidence interval **spanning zero under both proxies**: `[-2.06, +3.11]` and
`[-1.44, +3.63]`.

Offering a same-category substitute is **statistically indistinguishable from
offering nothing at all.**

That is not a null result to be shrugged at. It is a policy instruction: roughly
a quarter of substitution offers are ritual. They cost picker time, they cost
the customer a decision, and they buy nothing measurable.

---

## What the data cannot settle, stated plainly

1. **Whether an accepted substitute was satisfactory or merely tolerated.** No
   source system records it. No proxy recovers it. This is not a gap to be
   closed with cleverness; it is an absence.
2. **Whether the picker's decision to offer is independent of the customer, the
   basket, or the hour.** It is almost certainly not, and nothing observable
   corrects for it.
3. **The counterfactual.** What would *this* customer have done had the item
   been in stock? Observational data has no access to it, at any sample size.

---

## What would settle it

In ascending order of cost, descending order of ambiguity:

1. **A one-question post-delivery survey on substituted orders.**
   *"Was this replacement okay? Y/N."* Cheapest instrument available. Replaces
   three proxies with a label. This should have existed before the dashboard did.
2. **Randomise the substitution POLICY** — pre-approved vs ask-at-the-door.
   Not the stock-out (which would be unethical and operationally insane), the
   *policy*. See `docs/06_experimentation.md`; that experiment exists precisely
   because this chapter cannot close.
3. **Instrument the picker decision.** Log *why* a substitute was or was not
   offered. Turns an unobserved confounder into a covariate.

---

## The honest summary

I was asked to measure substitution quality. Instead I am reporting that:

- the metric requested **does not exist and cannot be constructed**;
- three reasonable constructions **disagree by 4.6 pp**;
- the **ranking is stable** and answers the actual operational question;
- roughly **a quarter of our substitution offers do nothing measurable**;
- and a **one-question survey** would replace this entire document.

That is a less satisfying deliverable than a number on a dashboard. It is a more
honest one, and it leads to a decision the number would not have.
